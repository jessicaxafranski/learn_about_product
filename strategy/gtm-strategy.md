# GTM Strategy — Go-to-Market for Product Managers

A Go-to-Market strategy is the plan that defines how a product reaches its target customers and competes in the market. It connects product, marketing, sales, and customer success into a single execution plan.

GTM is not just a launch checklist. It is the answer to: **who are we selling to, what are we saying, through which channels, at what price, and how do we grow from there.**

---

## When You Need a GTM Strategy

| Situation | Why GTM matters |
|---|---|
| **New product launch** | No existing customers or brand recognition to rely on |
| **New market or segment** | Different buyers, different language, different channels |
| **New pricing model** | Changes how customers perceive value and compare alternatives |
| **Major feature release** | Needs positioning to avoid being lost in release noise |
| **Competitive response** | Market shift requires re-positioning existing products |

---

## The Core GTM Questions

Every GTM strategy must answer six questions before execution begins.

| # | Question | What it produces |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Who is the customer? | ICP and persona definition |
| 2 | What problem do we solve? | Value proposition |
| 3 | Why us and not the alternative? | Positioning and differentiation |
| 4 | How do customers find and buy? | Channel and sales motion |
| 5 | What do they pay? | Pricing and packaging |
| 6 | How do we grow after the first sale? | Retention and expansion strategy |

---

## Step 1 — Define the ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)

The ICP is a description of the company or person most likely to buy, derive value, and stay. Without it, messaging is vague and sales cycles are long.

**For B2B products:**

| Dimension | Example |
|---|---|
| **Industry** | SaaS, Healthcare, Financial Services |
| **Company size** | 50–500 employees |
| **Geography** | North America, English-speaking |
| **Buyer role** | VP of Engineering, Head of Product |
| **Pain signal** | Growing team, recent compliance incident, scaling issues |

**For B2C products:**

| Dimension | Example |
|---|---|
| **Demographics** | 25–40, urban, professional |
| **Behavior** | Daily app user, early adopter |
| **Motivation** | Saves time, reduces anxiety, signals status |
| **Barrier** | Price sensitivity, habit inertia, trust |

> The ICP is not everyone who could use your product. It is the person for whom your product works so well that they become your best case study.

---

## Step 2 — Value Proposition

The value proposition is a single statement that connects your product's capability to the customer's problem, in language the customer actually uses.

**Structure:**
```
For [target customer]
who [has this problem or goal],
[product name] is a [category]
that [key benefit].
Unlike [alternative],
we [key differentiator].
```

**What makes a value proposition weak:**
- Describes features instead of outcomes ("We have AI-powered analytics")
- Uses internal language the customer never says
- Claims too many benefits — tries to be everything to everyone
- Is true of every competitor in the category

**What makes it strong:**
- Specific customer, specific pain
- Measurable outcome ("reduces onboarding time by 40%")
- Clear contrast with the status quo or alternative

---

## Step 3 — Positioning

Positioning is the place your product occupies in the customer's mind relative to alternatives. It is not your tagline — it is the internal framework that generates your tagline, your messaging, and your sales narrative.

**The April Dunford framework:**

| Element | Question |
|---|---|
| **Competitive alternatives** | What does the customer do if your product doesn't exist? |
| **Unique attributes** | What do you have that alternatives don't? |
| **Value** | What does that capability enable for the customer? |
| **Customer segments** | Who cares about that value most? |
| **Market frame** | What category puts your strengths in the best light? |

**Common positioning mistakes:**

- Positioning against a competitor instead of against the status quo
- Choosing a category that makes you look like the weaker option
- Saying you are "better" without specifying better at what, for whom

---

## Step 4 — Pricing and Packaging

Pricing is a positioning decision as much as a revenue decision. How you charge communicates who you are for.

**Common SaaS models:**

| Model | How it works | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Per seat** | Price × number of users | Collaboration tools |
| **Usage-based** | Pay for what you consume | Infrastructure, APIs |
| **Tiered** | Free / Pro / Enterprise | Wide range of customer sizes |
| **Flat fee** | One price for full access | Simple, predictable products |
| **Freemium** | Free core, paid upgrades | High-volume consumer or PLG |

**Pricing principles for PMs:**

- Price to value, not to cost
- Your lowest tier sets expectations about what the product is worth
- Enterprise pricing is almost always about risk reduction, not features
- Freemium works when free users create value for paid users (virality, network effects)

---

## Step 5 — Channels

Channels define how customers discover, evaluate, and buy your product. The right channel depends on deal size, buyer behavior, and your stage.

| Channel | Best for | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| **Product-led (PLG)** | Low ACV, self-serve, viral potential | Requires product to sell itself |
| **Inbound (content, SEO)** | Educated buyers, longer consideration | Slow to build, compounding returns |
| **Outbound (SDR/AE)** | High ACV, specific ICP, enterprise | Expensive, requires sales team |
| **Partnerships** | Access to existing customer base | Revenue share, slower control |
| **Marketplace** | Where buyers already shop (App Store, AWS) | Dependent on platform rules |
| **Community** | Developer tools, niche products | Hard to manufacture, high trust |

> Channel-product fit matters as much as product-market fit. A great product in the wrong channel fails.

---

## Step 6 — Launch Phases

A GTM launch is not a single event. It is a sequence with distinct goals at each stage.

```
Alpha → Beta → Limited Launch → General Availability (GA)
```

| Phase | Who | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Alpha** | Internal users | Catch critical bugs, validate core flow |
| **Beta** | Invited customers | Validate value proposition with real users |
| **Limited launch** | ICP segment only | Test messaging, pricing, and conversion |
| **GA** | Full market | Scale what works from limited launch |

**At each phase, define:**
- Who is included and why
- What success looks like (metric)
- What would cause you to pause or roll back

---

## GTM Metrics

| Stage | Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Reach, impressions, share of voice | Is the message getting out? |
| **Acquisition** | Signups, leads, trials | Is the channel working? |
| **Activation** | Users who hit the "aha moment" | Is the product delivering on the promise? |
| **Retention** | Day 7 / Day 30 retention, churn | Is the value sustainable? |
| **Revenue** | ARR, ACV, conversion rate | Is the business model working? |
| **Referral** | NPS, referrals, organic growth | Are customers selling for you? |

---

## The PM's Role in GTM

The PM does not own GTM alone — but they are the connective tissue.

| Who | Owns | PM's role |
|---|---|---|
| **Product** | What we're launching | Define scope, readiness criteria, rollback plan |
| **Marketing** | Messaging and demand gen | Validate ICP, review positioning, provide proof points |
| **Sales** | Pipeline and conversion | Build enablement materials, explain differentiation |
| **Customer Success** | Retention and expansion | Define onboarding, identify expansion triggers |
| **Design** | Launch assets and UX | Align on value prop language in product itself |

**What PMs typically get wrong in GTM:**

- Treating launch as the end of the work instead of the beginning
- Writing positioning that is accurate but not compelling
- Not defining what "success" looks like before launch — then claiming it retroactively
- Skipping beta because the timeline is tight — then getting surprised by the feedback at GA

## Common GTM Frameworks

| Framework | Best for |
|---|---|
| **Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)** | Understanding why customers hire your product |
| **Crossing the Chasm** | Moving from early adopters to mainstream market |
| **Product-Led Growth (PLG)** | When the product drives its own acquisition and expansion |
| **Demand Waterfall** | B2B funnel from awareness to closed revenue |
| **RICE** | Prioritizing which GTM initiatives to run first |

## GTM in Practice — Sponsored Channel ROI

This example shows how a job board platform measures the ROI of its sponsored listing channel across different employers and industries.

### Pricing Model

| Plan | Cost |
|---|---|
| **Pay-per-Click** | $0.25 per click (varies by job) |
| **Pay-per-Application** | $10 per application |

> **Note:** Part-time roles (Retail, Technology) do not have retention fields. For retail, many candidates deliver physical CVs — lower digital intent, salary range typically $30–50k.

### Campaign Performance Data

| Job ID | Employer Size | Location | Industry | Salary Range | Clicks | Applications | Budget | Cost/Click | ROI |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Small | New York, NY | Technology | $100k–120k | 2,000 | 150 | $500 | $0.25 | 36.5% |
| 3 | Small | Austin, TX | Healthcare | $80k–90k | 1,500 | 100 | $300 | $0.20 | 19% |
| 4 | Medium | Seattle, WA | Technology | $120k–140k | 2,500 | 200 | $700 | $0.28 | 55% |

### ROI Formula

```
ROI = ((Applications × $50) − Sponsored_Budget) / Sponsored_Budget × 100
```

- **Applications × $50** — estimated average value per application to the employer
- **− Sponsored_Budget** — subtracts the campaign cost
- **÷ Sponsored_Budget** — divides net profit by investment
- **× 100** — converts to percentage

### Key Observations

- **Job 4 (Seattle, Medium company, Tech)** delivers the highest ROI at 55% despite the highest budget — more clicks converted into applications efficiently
- **Job 3 (Austin, Healthcare)** has the lowest ROI — fewer applications per click suggests either a positioning or targeting issue
- Cost per click varies by job (0.20–0.28), which signals that demand and competition differ by market and industry
- A PM using this data would investigate **why Healthcare underperforms**: is it the salary range, the location, the messaging, or the audience targeting?

---

## Simple Mental Model

> GTM = right message + right person + right channel + right moment
> If any one of these is wrong, the others don't matter.
