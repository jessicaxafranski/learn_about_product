# Critical Thinking

Critical thinking is the practice of questioning and evaluating information for reliability, relevance, and validity before acting on it. For PMs, it's not a soft skill — it's how you avoid building the wrong thing with confidence.

The core principle: **evidence over authority**. If someone senior says something is true, that's not a reason to believe it. Challenge the claim with the same rigor you'd apply to anyone else.

---

## Question Types

Good critical thinking starts with asking the right kind of question. These fall into three categories:

**Clarification questions** — check that you've understood correctly before going further:
- Is that what you meant?
- Could you give me a concrete example?

**Evidence and reasoning questions** — probe why someone believes what they believe:
- What led you to that conclusion?
- What data supports this?
- What would change your mind?

**Implication questions** — explore the downstream consequences:
- If that's true, what would follow?
- What are we trading off if we go in that direction?
- What happens if we're wrong about this?

---

## The 7 Elements of Reasoning

This framework (adapted from Paul & Elder's critical thinking model) helps you examine any claim or decision systematically.

| Element | What to examine |
|---------|----------------|
| **Purpose** | Why are we doing this? Is the goal clear and shared? |
| **Questions** | Are we asking the right questions, or just the easy ones? |
| **Assumptions** | What are we taking for granted? How long can we safely assume this holds? |
| **Perspective** | Whose point of view is missing from this conversation? |
| **Information** | Is the data we're using relevant, current, and complete? |
| **Concepts** | Do we all mean the same thing when we use this term? |
| **Conclusions** | Given everything above, what's the most defensible interpretation? |

Run any major product decision through these seven lenses before committing.

## Reflective Skepticism

Before accepting a claim — from a stakeholder, a metric, a user interview, or an AI tool — run it through this checklist:

1. Is this a fact, or an opinion presented as one?
2. How relevant is this to the actual decision we're making?
3. Is the source credible, and do they have an incentive to mislead?
4. Is the claim ambiguous — could it mean something different to different people?
5. What assumptions are baked into this argument?
6. Is there a logical fallacy here (correlation presented as causation, survivorship bias, etc.)?
7. Are there inconsistencies between this claim and other things we know?
8. How strong is the overall argument, and what would weaken it?

The goal isn't to be contrarian. It's to arrive at better decisions by being honest about what you actually know versus what you're assuming.

## Applying This as a PM

Critical thinking is most valuable in three situations:

**During discovery** — when you're deciding which problems to investigate, skepticism prevents you from anchoring on the first user complaint you hear.

**During prioritization** — when stakeholders push for features, asking for evidence of impact protects the roadmap from HiPPO-driven decisions (Highest Paid Person's Opinion).

**During retrospectives** — when a launch goes well or badly, rigorous post-mortems separate luck from skill and help you learn the right lessons.

The most important habit: **write your assumptions down before you start**. When the outcome is known, it's easy to rationalize that you "knew all along." Documented assumptions hold you honest.
