# How to Create Key Results (KRs)

Key Results are the measurable outcomes that define what success looks like for a given Objective. In the OKR framework, the Objective answers *where you're going* and the KRs answer *how you'll know you've arrived*.

A KR is not a task ("launch the feature"), a project ("complete the redesign"), or an activity ("run user interviews"). It's an outcome — something that changes in the world as a result of your work.

---

## What Makes a KR Good

A well-written Key Result has these properties:

**Measurable.** The result must be expressed as a number, a percentage, a threshold, or a clearly binary state. "Improve user satisfaction" is not a KR. "Increase CSAT score from 3.8 to 4.2" is.

**Time-bound.** A KR without a deadline is a wish. Specify the quarter, sprint, or date by which the target should be reached.

**Outcome-oriented.** Focus on the change in user behavior, business performance, or system state — not on the activities that produce it.

**Ambitious but grounded.** KRs should require real effort and stretch the team. If you're confident you'll hit 100% of your KRs every quarter, they're too easy.

**Independently verifiable.** Anyone on the team should be able to look at the data and agree on whether the KR was met. Ambiguity in the measurement method is a red flag.

---

## The 15 Characteristics of a Strong Indicator

When designing the metric behind a KR, evaluate it against these criteria:

1. **Relevance** — Does this indicator actually connect to the objective, or is it a proxy that might diverge?
2. **Credibility** — Would stakeholders trust this number if they saw it? Is the data collection reliable?
3. **Proportionate effort** — Is the cost of measuring this worth the value of the information?
4. **Timeliness** — Can you get this data fast enough to act on it, or is it always lagging?
5. **Actionability** — If the number moves in the wrong direction, do you know what to do about it?
6. **Clarity** — Is the definition of the metric written down and unambiguous?
7. **Comparability** — Can you compare this metric across time periods, segments, or product lines?
8. **Granularity** — Is the metric broken down at the right level to surface useful insights?
9. **Accessibility** — Is the data available to everyone who needs it, or gated behind a specific team?
10. **Consistency** — Is it calculated the same way every time, by every person?
11. **Sensitivity** — Does the metric move when something meaningful happens, or is it too coarse?
12. **Resistance to gaming** — Can people improve the number without improving the underlying reality?
13. **Alignment** — Does improving this metric align with improving the overall business or user outcome?
14. **Sustainability** — Can you track this metric indefinitely, or will the cost of collection grow over time?
15. **Visibility** — Is this metric surfaced where teams can see it regularly, not buried in a report?

No single KR will perfectly satisfy all 15. This list is a tool for identifying weaknesses before you commit, not a checklist that must be fully satisfied.

## Common KR Mistakes

**Writing outputs instead of outcomes.** "Ship the onboarding redesign" is a task. "Increase onboarding completion rate from 42% to 65%" is a KR.

**Setting too many KRs.** Three to five per Objective is the practical ceiling. More than that and the team can't hold them all in focus — and trade-offs between KRs become invisible.

**Ignoring leading indicators.** Lagging indicators (revenue, retention) tell you what happened. Leading indicators (activation rate, feature adoption) tell you what's likely to happen. Mix both.

**Forgetting the baseline.** If you don't know where you're starting from, you can't set a meaningful target. Measure first, then set the KR.

---

## Template

Use this structure when drafting a KR:

> **We will know we've succeeded when** [metric] **moves from** [current baseline] **to** [target] **by** [date].

Example:
> We will know we've succeeded when the 30-day retention rate for new users moves from 28% to 40% by end of Q3.
