# Why Data-Driven Decisions Matter

Most product decisions feel like they're based on data. In practice, they're often based on the story someone tells *about* the data — which is a different thing. Building a genuine data-driven practice means being honest about that gap and closing it deliberately.

---

## What "Data-Driven" Actually Means

Being data-driven doesn't mean requiring a spreadsheet for every decision. It means that when you make a call, you can articulate:

- What evidence supports it
- What evidence would have changed it
- What assumptions you're still relying on

A decision made without being able to answer those three questions is an opinion, not a data-driven choice — even if there's a dashboard somewhere with numbers on it.

---

## Why It Matters for Product Teams

**Better accuracy.** Intuition is shaped by recent experience, loud stakeholders, and cognitive bias. Data forces you to confront what's actually happening across your full user base, not just the cases you've seen.

**Faster decisions.** Counterintuitively, a team with clear metrics and a shared definition of success moves faster — not slower. Debates end when there's agreed-upon evidence to consult.

**Understanding customers at scale.** You can't talk to every user. Data is how you learn what the users you *didn't* talk to are doing, where they're dropping off, and what they value.

**Accountability.** When you define the metric before launching a feature, you're committing to a falsifiable prediction. That's uncomfortable, but it's also the only way to actually learn whether something worked.

**Informed prioritization.** Without data, every stakeholder's priority feels equally urgent. With it, you can show the relative impact of different bets and make trade-offs visible.

---

## Common Failure Modes

Knowing the risks is as important as knowing the benefits:

- **Metric fixation:** Optimizing for a metric until it stops representing what you actually care about. (Classic example: optimizing for clicks at the expense of satisfaction.)
- **Confirmation bias:** Only pulling data that supports a decision you've already made.
- **Vanity metrics:** Reporting numbers that look good but don't connect to outcomes — total signups, page views, app downloads.
- **Analysis paralysis:** Waiting for perfect data before making any decision. Good enough data, decided on quickly, usually beats perfect data arrived at too late.

---

## A Practical Starting Point

If your team isn't data-driven yet, start with three steps:

1. **Define success before you build.** Before any feature ships, write down: "This feature will be considered successful if [metric] moves from [X] to [Y] within [Z] weeks."

2. **Instrument the thing you care about.** You can't measure what you haven't tracked. Make sure the events, funnels, and cohorts you need exist before launch — not after.

3. **Review data at a fixed cadence.** A weekly 30-minute metrics review, consistently attended, builds more data literacy in a team than any training course.

Data doesn't make decisions for you. It narrows down the space of defensible decisions and helps you make better bets.
