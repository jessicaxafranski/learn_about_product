# Performance Indicators

A performance indicator is a quantitative or qualitative measure that tells you whether a process or product is achieving its intended outcome. Without indicators, you're navigating without instruments — you might feel like things are going well right up until they aren't.

---

## The Core Question

Before choosing an indicator, answer this: **what decision will this metric inform?**

If you can't name a concrete decision that the metric would change, it's probably a vanity metric — something that feels useful to measure but doesn't actually drive action.

---

## Types of Indicators

### Trend indicators

These are forward-looking. They measure early signals that predict future outcomes. Companies use them to take **preventive action** before a problem fully materializes.

Examples: Support ticket volume (predicts churn), feature adoption rate in the first week (predicts long-term retention), sales pipeline coverage (predicts revenue shortfall).

### Performance indicators

These are backward-looking. They confirm whether a goal was met after the fact. They tell you the **efficacy of past decisions**.

Examples: Monthly revenue, quarterly retention, NPS score.

Most dashboards overindex on performance indicators because they're easier to define. The most actionable teams also track trend indicators because they give you time to respond.

---

## Efficiency vs. Effectiveness

These two concepts are frequently confused and frequently misused:

| | Efficiency | Effectiveness |
|--|------------|---------------|
| **Definition** | Doing things the right way; optimizing resource use | Doing the right things; achieving the intended goal |
| **Question it answers** | Are we minimizing waste? | Are we hitting our targets? |
| **Example** | Reducing cost-per-ticket in customer support | Resolving customer issues on the first contact |
| **Measured by** | Productivity metrics, cost ratios | Goal attainment, outcome metrics |

A team can be highly efficient (low cost, fast execution) while being ineffective (building the wrong thing). The goal is to be both — and performance indicators should be designed to catch failures on either dimension.

---

## Defining Your Measurement Framework

Every indicator needs three supporting elements to be useful:

**Objective** — the high-level direction you're moving in ("improve customer experience").

**Goal** — the specific, quantifiable target that defines success ("increase first-contact resolution rate to 85%").

**Deadline** — the date by which the goal should be reached. Without this, goals become aspirations.

| Objective | Goal | Deadline |
|-----------|------|----------|
| Increase revenue | Grow MRR from €80K to €100K | End of Q2 |
| Reduce churn | Decrease monthly churn from 5% to 3% | End of Q3 |
| Improve onboarding | Increase activation rate from 40% to 60% | End of Q1 |

These three elements must stay connected. A goal without an objective loses strategic context. A goal without a deadline is unmeasurable. An objective without a goal is a direction, not a commitment.

---

## Data vs. Information

This distinction matters more than it seems:

**Data** is raw — numbers, events, logs. It exists in databases. By itself it doesn't mean anything.

**Information** is data that has been processed and interpreted to support a decision. Information is what your indicator actually surfaces.

The job of a performance indicator is to transform data into information. A metric that nobody reads or acts on is just data.

---

## Quantitative vs. Qualitative Indicators

**Quantitative indicators** produce numerical results: percentages, counts, ratios, averages. They're easy to track and compare over time.

**Qualitative indicators** produce non-numerical results: sentiment, satisfaction categories, pass/fail judgments. They're harder to aggregate but often capture nuance that numbers miss.

Most mature measurement systems use both. NPS, for example, is quantitative — but the open-text follow-up is qualitative, and often more informative.

---

## KPIs

A **Key Performance Indicator** is simply a performance indicator that the organization has decided is *critical* — directly tied to the most important business outcomes. Not every metric is a KPI.

The test: if this KPI moved significantly in the wrong direction, would leadership change priorities? If yes, it's a KPI. If no, it's just a metric.

---

## Performance Scale

When comparing results across different KPIs (which may have different units), normalize them to a performance score:

| Outcome | Performance |
|---------|-------------|
| All objectives achieved | 100% |
| Objectives partially achieved | < 100% |
| Objectives exceeded | > 100% |

Aiming for 70–80% on ambitious targets is often healthier than consistently hitting 100% — consistent 100% usually means targets were set too low.
