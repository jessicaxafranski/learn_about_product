# AI Biases and Critical Thinking

*Based on the LinkedIn course: Amplify Your Critical Thinking with Generative AI.*

AI tools are useful, but they're not neutral. They reflect the data they were trained on, which means they carry the biases, gaps, and distortions present in that data. Using AI without understanding this leads to confident-sounding outputs built on flawed foundations.

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Confirmation bias** | The tendency to seek out or accept only information that confirms existing beliefs, while ignoring contradictory evidence |
| **Historical bias** | Bias that arises because AI training data reflects historical patterns — including historical inequalities and outdated norms |
| **Critical thinking** | Questioning and evaluating information for reliability, relevance, and validity before acting on it |
| **Skepticism** | An active, engaged form of doubt — not cynicism, but a deliberate refusal to accept claims without examination |
| **Truth claim** | A statement presented as fact that actually requires context and is subject to legitimate debate |
| **PIQPACC** | A critical thinking framework: Purpose, Information, Questions, Perspectives, Assumptions, Concepts, Conclusions |

---

## Historical Bias in AI

AI models are trained on large corpora of text from the internet, books, and other sources. Those sources reflect the world as it was — including historical inequalities, outdated professional norms, and the biases of whoever produced the content.

This means that when you ask an AI a question about careers, markets, or demographics, the answer may be shaped by data that is years out of date or that reflects patterns that no longer hold.

**Questions to surface historical bias:**
- Is the information you're providing based on recent data, or could older training data be influencing it?
- Does the career advice you're giving reflect current market conditions, or historical trends?
- When you describe typical outcomes for [role/group], what time period does that reflect?

For product managers specifically: AI salary benchmarks, hiring trend data, and competitive landscape analysis may all be affected by historical bias. Treat them as starting points, not authoritative sources.

---

## Confirmation Bias — In Humans and AI

Confirmation bias doesn't just affect the AI — it affects how you use it. If you ask AI to validate a decision you've already made, you'll get validation. The AI will find supporting arguments for almost any position, because it's designed to be responsive and helpful.

This is dangerous in product work because it feels like research but produces the same outcome you would have reached without it.

**Strategies to counter this:**

Ask the AI to argue the other side:
> "What are the strongest arguments *against* this product direction?"

Ask it to find disconfirming evidence:
> "What data might suggest this bet won't pay off?"

Ask it to surface assumptions:
> "What would have to be true for this strategy to succeed? What's most likely to be wrong?"

## Applying the PIQPACC Framework to AI Output

When evaluating anything an AI produces — analysis, research, recommendations — run it through these seven lenses:

| Element | What to ask |
|---------|------------|
| **Purpose** | What is this information being used for? Does the AI's framing match my actual question? |
| **Information** | Where did this come from? What sources does it reflect? How current is it? |
| **Questions** | Am I asking the right question, or am I getting a precise answer to a vague question? |
| **Perspectives** | Whose viewpoint is represented here? Who is missing? |
| **Assumptions** | What has the AI taken for granted? What would change the answer if it weren't true? |
| **Concepts** | Are key terms being used consistently and accurately? |
| **Conclusions** | Does the conclusion follow from the evidence, or is it the AI filling a gap with a plausible-sounding guess? |

---

## Practical Prompts for More Reliable AI Output

These prompts are designed to force the AI to be more transparent about its limitations:

- "What are the limitations of the answer you just gave me?"
- "How confident are you in this, and what would increase or decrease that confidence?"
- "What information would you need to give a better answer?"
- "Are there perspectives or data sources that would contradict what you just said?"
- "Is this based on recent data, or are you drawing on older information?"

The goal isn't to distrust AI — it's to use it as a thinking partner that helps you reason better, not as an oracle that relieves you of the need to think.
