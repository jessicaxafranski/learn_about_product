# Case Interview Guide

Case interviews for Product Manager roles test how you think, not just what you know. Interviewers are looking for structure, judgment, and the ability to make reasonable calls under uncertainty — not perfect answers.

---

## What Interviewers Are Actually Evaluating

**Can you structure ambiguous problems?** Open-ended questions are intentionally vague. The ability to break them into components — and communicate that breakdown clearly — is the core skill being tested.

**Do you have strong product instincts?** PMs rarely have all the data they'd like. Interviewers want to see that you can make sound decisions from incomplete information and explain your reasoning.

**Are you driving or being driven?** The best candidates lead the conversation — they ask clarifying questions, state their approach before diving in, narrate their thinking, and surface trade-offs. Avoid waiting to be guided.

---

## How to Structure Your Approach

1. **Clarify the question** — before answering, make sure you understand the actual problem. Asking a clarifying question is a signal of rigor, not uncertainty.
2. **State your framework** — tell the interviewer how you're going to approach it before you start. "I want to think about this in three parts: the user segment, their pain points, and potential solutions."
3. **Go broad before going deep** — explore the space before committing to one direction.
4. **Make explicit trade-offs** — don't just pick a solution, explain why you're prioritizing this over alternatives.
5. **Tie back to metrics** — what does success look like, and how would you measure it?

---

## Useful Frameworks

You don't need to memorize these — you need to understand when each one is appropriate.

| Framework | Best used for |
|-----------|--------------|
| **SWOT** (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) | Strategic positioning, competitor analysis |
| **Porter's Five Forces** | Assessing industry competition and market attractiveness |
| **4Ps / Marketing Mix** (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) | Go-to-market strategy, pricing decisions |
| **AIDA** (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action) | Customer acquisition and conversion funnel design |
| **REAN** (Reach, Engage, Activate, Nurture) | Digital marketing campaign planning |
| **Five Cs** (Company, Customers, Competitors, Collaborators, Context) | Full business environment analysis |

## Question Types and How to Handle Them

### Strategy Questions

These evaluate whether you can think at both the company level and the product level.

| Level | Sample Questions |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Micro** | What is the business model for this product? What would need to be true for it to succeed? Will customers want it? |
| **Macro** | How does this product fit into the company's overall vision? Does it open new markets or defend existing ones? |

For macro questions, frameworks like SWOT and Porter's Five Forces are useful. For micro questions, focus on the specific mechanics of the business model.

### Product Sense Questions

These test whether you can think about users rigorously and generate creative solutions.

**Structure your answer like this:**

1. **Motivation** — why should we build this? Is it aligned with the company's mission?
2. **User segmentation** — who specifically are we building for? Consider: size of the segment, frequency of use, willingness to pay.
3. **Pain points** — what specific problem does this user have? What are they doing today instead?
4. **Solutions** — what could we build? What's innovative here — not just a copy of something that already exists?

When proposing a solution, acknowledge if something similar already exists and explain what you'd add or improve. This is more impressive than pretending the space is blank.

### Launch Questions

| Stage | Key Questions |
|-------|--------------|
| **Product** | What is the core value proposition? Who is the target user? |
| **Launch Goals** | What does success look like? What are the KPIs? |
| **Launch Design** | What messaging will resonate? Which channels will you prioritize? |
| **Pre-launch** | How will you test? What beta feedback do you need? |
| **During launch** | How will you handle unexpected issues? Who owns what? |
| **Post-launch** | How will you measure results? What will you iterate on? |

### Pricing Questions

| Strategy | When to use it |
|----------|----------------|
| **Cost-plus** | Stable, predictable costs; low price sensitivity |
| **Value-based** | Premium product with clear, differentiated value |
| **Competitive** | Commoditized market; differentiation is limited |
| **Experimental** | New product category; willingness-to-pay is unknown |

### Problem-Solving (Metric Drop) Questions

When a metric falls, don't jump to conclusions. Work through the problem systematically:

1. **Clarify the metric** — how is it defined, and over what timeframe did it drop?
2. **Check for external factors** — seasonality, outages, data pipeline issues, competitor moves
3. **Segment the data** — is the drop uniform, or concentrated in a specific platform, region, or user cohort?
4. **Hypothesize causes** — list possible explanations before investigating any of them
5. **Prioritize hypotheses** — which ones are most likely, and which ones are easiest to test?
6. **Recommend next steps** — what would you investigate first, and what would you do if that hypothesis is confirmed?

## What Not to Do

- Asking clarifying questions that won't change your answer (it signals you're buying time, not being rigorous)
- Assuming you're building for the company you're interviewing at — the question often describes a different product
- Jumping straight to a solution without framing the user and problem first
- Choosing a framework and filling it in mechanically without applying judgment
- Forgetting to discuss trade-offs — the best answers acknowledge what you're giving up

---

## Preparation Checklist

- [ ] Review SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, 4Ps, and RICE — know when to use each
- [ ] Practice a metric-drop question from scratch, out loud
- [ ] Practice a "design a product for X" question, timing yourself
- [ ] Write out 3–5 STAR stories from your experience that cover leadership, data-driven decisions, and stakeholder conflict
- [ ] Research the company's products, business model, and recent news before the interview
